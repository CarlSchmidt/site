<h3>
<a id="About" class="anchor" href="#About" aria-hidden="true"><span class="octicon octicon-link"></span></a>About</h3>
<p>I'm a Paris-based researcher at <a href="http://www.latmos.ipsl.fr/" class="LATMOS">LATMOS</a> with a concentration on planetary atmospheres. This site contains information about my various projects. </p>
<h3>
<a id="Mercury" class="anchor" href="#Mercury" aria-hidden="true"><span class="octicon octicon-link"></span></a>Mercury</h3>
<p>Mercury's atmospheric content is comparable to the air in a small building, spread over the whole planet. Still, the extreme brightness of the sodium D emission lines are easily observed via small ground-based telescopes. Interpreting such observations, alongside the extensive <a href="http://www.nasa.gov/mission_pages/messenger/main/">MESSENGER</a> dataset, however requires complex numerical models and so their development is currently my main project.</p>
<h3>
<a id="Comets" class="anchor" href="#Comets" aria-hidden="true"><span class="octicon octicon-link"></span></a>Comets</h3>
<p>Cometary atmospheres are rich in complexity. Using an integral field spectrograph I've studied the composition, structure, and dynamics of Comet C/2012 S1 (ISON), which, well. . . fell into the Sun. I'm also part of a group using the STEREO spacecraft to observe striae formation in Comet C/2011 L4 (Pan-STARRS), which is alive and well.</p>
<h3>
<a id="Io" class="anchor" href="#Io" aria-hidden="true"><span class="octicon octicon-link"></span></a>Io</h3>
<p>Jupiter's volcanic moon Io is a very interesting object. I've use the infrared methods to study volcanoes on it's surface, small telescopes at McDonald Observatory to observe the sodium atmospheric escape, and the DIS spectrograph at Apache Point Observatory to track a wobbly doughnut orbiting Jupiter called the Io Plasma Torus.</p>
<h3>
<a id="Europa" class="anchor" href="#Europa" aria-hidden="true"><span class="octicon octicon-link"></span></a>Europa</h3>
<p>Io can potenitally transfer some of it's massively extended atmosphere to an adjacent moon, Europa. I'm currently working on a better understanding of the fraction of the sodium in Europa's atmosphere that is transferred from Io, rather than of native origins. Addressing this question ultimately helps solve another; what is the salinity of Europa's subsurface ocean?</p>
<h3>
<a id="Moon" class="anchor" href="#Earth's-Moon" aria-hidden="true"><span class="octicon octicon-link"></span></a>Moon</h3>
<p>Earth's moon also has a sodium atmosphere quite similar to Mercury's. During the <a href="http://lcross.arc.nasa.gov/impact.htm">LCROSS</a> impact, colleagues at McDonald Observatory made spectral observations of a plume of sodium extending some 200 km above the impact site. A simulation of these data is planned, for comparison with Mercury where no sodium signature of the <a href="http://www.nasa.gov/mission_pages/messenger/main/">MESSENGER</a> impact was observed.</p>
