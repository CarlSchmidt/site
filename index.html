About

I'm a Paris-based researcher at LATMOS with a concentration on planetary atmospheres. This site contains information about my various projects.

Mercury

Mercury's atmospheric content is comparable to the air in a small building, spread over the whole planet. Still, the extreme brightness of the sodium D emission lines are easily observed via small ground-based telescopes. Interpreting such observations, alongside the extensive MESSENGER dataset, requires complex numerical models and so their development is currently my main project.

Comets

Cometary atmosphere's are rich in complexity. Using an integral field spectrograph I study on the composition, structure, and dynamics of Comet C/2012 S1 (ISON), which, well. . . fell into the Sun. I'm also part of a group using the STEREO spacecraft to observe striae formation in Comet C/2011 L4 (Pan-STARRS), which is alive and active.

Io

Jupiter's volcanic moon Io is a very interesting object. I've use the infrared methods to study volcanoes on it's surface, small telescopes at McDonald Observatory to observe the sodium atmospheric escape, and the DIS spectrograph at Apache Point Observatory to track a wobbly doughnut orbiting Jupiter called the Io Plasma Torus.

Europa

Io can potenitally transfer some of it's massively extended atmosphere to an adjacent moon, Europa. I'm currently working on a better understanding of the fraction of the sodium in Europa's atmosphere that is transferred from Io, rather than of native origin. Addressing this question ultimately helps solve another; what is the salinity of Europa's subsurface ocean?

Moon

Earth's moon also has a sodium atmosphere quite similar to Mercury's. During the LCROSS impact, colleagues at McDonald Observatory made spectral observations of a plume of sodium extending some 200 km above the impact site. I'm also working on a simulation of these data, compared with Mercury where no sodium signature of the MESSENGER impact was observed.
